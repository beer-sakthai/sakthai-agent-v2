# B2B Deal Packaging & Professional Proposal Pricing

## When to Use This Skill

- Preparing an enterprise SOW with >1-year term or >$50k TCV
- Pricing a multi-year renewal to lock in expansion while maintaining margin
- Structuring an ADD-ON or platform deal that stacks modules, seats, and services
- Negotiating against procurement’s anchor and need a disciplined concession plan
- Building enterprise proposal templates that survive procurement scrutiny and legal review
- Training AEs on proposal structure before they get into the field

---

## Core Principle

> **Deal packaging is the art of making the buyer feel like they won while you capture
> maximum TCV and margin.**
>
> In enterprise B2B, the buyer will *always* negotiate. The deal is won or lost in
> how you frame choices and manage concessions—not in the first number you quote.

---

## Step 1 — Diagnose the Deal Architecture

Map the deal before you price it.

| Component           | Questions to Answer                                                        |
|---------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Core product        | What is the primary value metric (seats, usage, credit)?                   |
| ADD-ONs / modules   | What adjacent capabilities increase time-to-value?                         |
| Services            | Implementation, training, CSM, premium support tiers?                     |
| Multi-year dynamics | Is there a preferred commitment window (annual vs. 3-year)?              |
| Expansion signals   | Does the buyer express intent to grow seats/usage over the term?          |

**Deal archetypes:**
- **Land & Expand:** Low initial commitment, high expansion intent → maximize annual flexibility, front-load success milestones
- **Platform consolidation:** Buyer replacing 2+ vendors → bundle for switching cost, price on net value
- **RFP / Procurement:** Competing bids, decision matrix → differentiation on TCO, risk reduction, implementation speed
- **New market wedge:** Unknown buyer/brand → price for referenceability and case-study rights

## Step 2 — TCV vs ACV Framing

Enterprise buyers see budget in **Total Contract Value (TCV)**; operators track **ACV**.

```
TCV  = Σ (all services + software fees + third-party costs) over contract term
ACV  = TCV / contract term in years
```

**Framing rules:**
1. Lead with **annualized value** when comparing to their budget cycle (“This is $120k/yr, 3-year commitment”)
2. When negotiating multi-year, show **TCV discount** but keep **ACV floor** as your walk-away
3. Segment your proposal: Software (subscription), Services (implementation), Integration (one-time + recurring)
4. Never mix services and software in the same line item — procurement will discount services more aggressively

---

## Step 3 — The 3 Framework Tiers Inside Every Proposal

Regardless of product, every enterprise proposal should offer **three packaged options**:

| Tier                     | Goal                                                               | Pricing Anchor       |
|--------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------|
| **The Baseline**         | Meets stated requirements cleanly; defines the scope of “solving it” | Fair market / list   |
| **The Target (Recommended)** | Solves adjacent problems; includes services and integration         | 20–40% premium over Baseline |
| **The Strategic Anchor** | Full platform / 3-year committed / premium services tier            | 1.8–2.5× Target ACV  |

> Procurement will fight Baseline.
> Most stakeholders will gravitate to Target.
> Anchor makes Target look reasonable.

**The decoy must be real** — if the buyer asks for it, you must be able to deliver. The Anchor’s purpose is not to sell; it is to make Target feel like the “sensible middle.”

## Step 4 — ADD-ON and Multi-Year Structuring

### ADD-ON Stacking Strategy
```
Base           = Core license (required to do business)
Layer 1        = Critical enhancement (e.g., API access, advanced analytics)
Layer 2        = Growth enablement (e.g., community, additional modules)
Layer 3        = Premium services (dedicated CSM, 24/7 support, custom SLA)
```

Pricing rule: Each layer should cost **≥ 2× the prior layer** on a per-unit basis.
If Layer 1 = 20% of Base price, Layer 2 should be ≥ 40–50% of Base price.
This keeps upselling revenue-rich and prevents the buyer from treating all layers as commodities.

### Multi-Year Math
| Term | Typical ACV Premium vs. Monthly | Cash Flow Impact | Churn Reduction |
|------|--------------------------------|------------------|-----------------|
| 1 yr | 0% (baseline)                  | Baseline         | —               |
| 2 yr | 10–15% ACV uplift              | 2× annual upfront cash | +5–10 pts vs. 1-yr |
| 3 yr | 15–25% ACV uplift              | 3× upfront       | +10–15 pts vs. 1-yr |

**Contract mechanics:**
- Offer 12/24/36-month options with increasing ACV discount
- Tie 3-year pricing to **platform commitment** (i.e., they agree to >80% of current seats + growth corridor)
- Use **price protection** as a negotiation chip: “3-year lock at current rates if signed by [date]”
- Avoid pure “CPI cap” language; if you must, use it only for the service component, not software

## Step 5 — Negotiation Anchoring & Concession Sequencing

### Opening Anchor
Set the anchor **above** where you expect to land. In proposals, this is the Anchor tier price. In verbal negotiation, it is the first number out of your mouth.

> **Anchoring rule:** Whoever names a number first captures the negotiation range. Don’t let procurement anchor first.

### Concession Ladder
Plan your concessions in **three moves**, each shrinking:

```
Move 1 (20% of budget):   Free implementation / onboarding credit
Move 2 (10% of budget):   Extended payment terms (NDR 45 → 60 days)
Move 3 (5% of budget):    Additional user seats at current rate / QBR cadence
Reserve (3% of budget):   Final walk-away clause (auto-revert to baseline terms)
```

**Rules:**
1. Never give two concessions in the same round — match each concession to a specific ask
2. After you give Move 1, explicitly say: “That was the easy one. After Move 2 we’ll need [X] from you.”
3. Never concede **price** in the first three moves; concede **scope, payment terms, or SLA** first
4. End every concession with: “I need [an equivalent commitment from you]” (longer term, case study, reference)

## Step 6 — Discount Governance at the Deal Desk

### Approval Matrix

| Discount Level vs. ACS  | Approval Path                | Required Counters       |
|-------------------------|------------------------------|-------------------------|
| ≤ 15% off ACS           | AE discretion                | None                    |
| 15–25% off ACS          | Sales Manager                | Competitive intel doc   |
| 25–35% off ACS          | VP Sales                     | Value justification memo |
| 35–50% off ACS          | CRO + CFO                    | Legal addendum (price protection cap) |
| > 50% off ACS           | CEO                          | Board notification      |

**ACS** = Approved Contracted Software (the software portion after standard volume discounts).

### Exception Rules
- Never discount **outside** a quarter-end window without CFO sign-off
- All exceptions require a **real-time CRM override** (shadow list = audit trail)
- Discounts must come from either: competitive pressure, multi-year commit, immediate cash, or strategic reference — not “they asked”
- Track discount velocity by AE and segment in RevOps dashboards

## Step 7 — Proposal Writing Architecture

### Page Structure (8–12 pages for enterprise)

1. **Exec Summary (1 page):**
   - Three bullets: current pain, quantified impact, proposed solution
   - Single TCV/ACV summary box
   - “Recommended” badge on Target tier box

2. **Solution Overview (1–2 pages):**
   - How the product solves their stated problem
   - Differentiation from incumbents/alternatives in 3 bullets

3. **Three-Tier Comparison (1 page):**
   - Feature matrix (not exhaustive; highlight deltas)
   - Pricing table: Baseline / Target / Anchor
   - “Target” tier has ★ RECOMMENDED marker

4. **Terms & Pricing Detail (1–2 pages):**
   - Per-component line items (subscription, implementation, training)
   - Payment schedule tied to milestones (kickoff, delivery, acceptance)
   - Renewal clauses excluded; addressed in master agreement

5. **Implementation & Success (1 page):**
   - 30/60/90-day plan with named roles (CSM, TAM, SE)
   - Success metrics and quarterly business review cadence

6. **Legal / Commercial Terms (1–2 pages):**
   - Data security, SLA, liability cap, termination for cause vs. convenience
   - Auto-renewal clause and notification window (99 days pre-expiry recommended)

### Formatting Rules
- Font ≥ 11pt in body; pricing in **bold** in a box
- All dates in **UTC+local**; payment terms as net 30 unless negotiated otherwise
- Pixel-perfect PDF; avoid Word if possible (Perceptive loses deals on polish)
- Brand colors only on cover; use gray for body pages (appears more serious)

## Step 8 — Contract Mechanics That Protect Revenue

### Lock-in without Lock-in
Enterprises resist long lock-in. Use **serially renewable annual commitments** with:
- **Auto-renewal** that activates unless either party provides 90 days’ notice
- **Governance for exit:** “We will help you migrate data in 30 days”; don’t promise XML exports you can’t do
- **Rate protection:** If they leave, their successor license is at prevailing rate — not legacy

### Ramp Contracts
For new logos, accept ramps but structure as:
```
Year 1: 50 seats (full price)
Year 2: 100 seats (10% volume discount on incremental)
Year 3: 200 seats (15% volume discount on incremental)
```
- Ramp commitment **must be signed** and tied to an **annual contract**
- Ramp never excuses lower ACV; treat the full ramp TCV as committed ARR
- If buyer objects, move to shorter ramp + full-price baseline

### Holdbacks & Credits
- **Implementation holdback:** 10–15% of Year 1 fees reserved until go-live
- **Discount as credit:** “We’ll apply the 20% as a Year 2 credit if you hit $X usage by Month 12”

## Step 9 — Pricing Experiments in Enterprise Context

Unlike freemium / self-serve, enterprise pricing must be **more stable**; live price tests can destroy trust. Instead:

| Test Type            | Method                                              | Caution             |
|----------------------|-----------------------------------------------------|---------------------|
| Feature bundling     | A/B on which features appear in Baseline vs. Target | Small buyer cohort  |
| Payment schedule     | 100% upfront vs. quarterly vs. net-45               | Track DSO impact    |
| Anchor height        | Move Anchor tier from 2.0× to 2.5× Target           | Deals leak more; measure discount rate |
| Add-on sequencing    | Change which modules are pre-bundled in Target       | Compare attach rate |

**Enterprise rule:** Run experiments only in Q3/Q4 when pipeline is lighter; do not test pricing during the buyer’s evaluation committee.

## Step 10 — Common Deal Failures and Fixes

| Failure Symptom                   | Root Cause                                        | Fix |
|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|-----|
| Target tier converts <30%         | Baseline is too generous or Anchor missing        | Reduce Baseline feature set; add/differentiate Anchor |
| Procurement discounts >35%        | No formal approval matrix; AEs discounting alone | Enforce deal desk before first verbal discount |
| Buyer asks “can you go lower?” before seeing Target | Anchor was shown weak or missing   | Always show 3 in same view; never lead with Baseline |
| Add-on attach rate <15%           | Add-ons priced as premium options, not “required” | Rename Layer 1 as “Standard”; make Baseline incomplete |
| Multi-year close rate stalls      | No concrete value case for committing 3 years      | Build 3-year TCO model + price-protection headline |
| Post-sign expansion rate weak     | Contract didn’t reserve growth corridor           | Write growth-path discount schedule into original SOW |

## Verification Checklist

Before sending the proposal or finalizing the deal:

- [ ] Three tiers are visibly different in scope and price (not cosmetic)
- [ ] Target tier is recommended in writing and positioned as the “standard” solution
- [ ] ACV floor is documented in CRM as the discount except floor before deal desk
- [ ] Concession ladder is prepared and visible to AE + manager before first call
- [ ] Payment schedule has at least 25–50% of first-year fees invoiced at go-live
- [ ] Auto-renewal / notice clause is ≥ 90 days and legal-approved
- [ ] Implementation success criteria are named with measurable outcomes
- [ ] Expansion corridor (Year 2/3 seats/usage) is discussed, even if not contractually locked

---

## Operator Mindset

Enterprise buyers have budget cycles, procurement gatekeepers, and internal politics. The proposal is your **strategic artifact** — it must do three things simultaneously:
1. Show the buyer they can win internally (look smart to their boss)
2. Defend your price by framing alternatives and TCO
3. Create optionality for expansion without giving away the store

If you finish a proposal and it looks like a price sheet with a cover letter → redo it.
