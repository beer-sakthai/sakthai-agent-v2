# Small Deal M&A Due Diligence & Integration Playbook

## When to use
- Acquiring a business under $25M annual revenue (SMB, micro-SaaS, services firm, vertical SaaS).
- Roll-up / operator-led growth where speed and operational fit matter more than giant premiums.
- Need a repeatable, board-ready diligence workbook rather than one-off legal/financial review.

## The R.E.M.A.P. Diligence Frame
Walk every deal through these five lenses. Skip none; spend more time where risk is highest.

| Axis | What to review | Key questions | Documents to request |
|------|----------------|----------------|----------------------|
| **R**evenue Quality | Is the revenue real, recurring, and durable? | Customer concentration >10%? Contract terms? Rebilling / channel stuffing? | 12-months AR, customer list, top-10 invoices, contracts |
| **E**arnings Power | What does the business actually earn on a normalized basis? | Owner perks? Non-recurring gains? Related-party payments? | P&L, tax returns, owner add-backs schedule, payroll register |
| **M**argin Trajectory | Are margins stable, improving, or declining? | Gross margin by product? EBITDA margin trend? | Monthly trended P&L, COGS detail, unit economics if SaaS |
| **A**ssets | What do you actually get? | IP assignment? Customer data portability? Key equipment? | Cap table, IP register, asset register, leases |
| **P**eople | Who must stay for post-close value? | Key person risk? One-person revenue? | Org chart, employment agreements, non-competes, owner transition plan |

## Intensity Grid
Classify every deal into one of four profiles to set diligence depth and speed targets.

| Profile | Revenue range | Diligence days | Typical docs | Deal size ($) |
|---------|---------------|----------------|--------------|---------------|
| **Micro** | <$1M | 5–10 | 2 years tax + P&L + bank statements | <$500K |
| **Small** | $1M–$5M | 10–20 | 3 years tax + P&L + cap table + customer list | $500K–$3M |
| **Mid** | $5M–$15M | 20–40 | QOQs, audited best-effort, CIM quality info | $3M–$10M |
| **Growth** | $15M–$25M | 40–70 | Full QOQs, QoE, legal DD, IP review | $10M–$25M |

## Valuation Cheat Sheet
Use conservative caps to avoid bidding wars and integration debt.

| Method | Formula / Rule |
|--------|----------------|
| **Revenue multiple** | 0.5×–2.0× revenue depending on growth %, churn, and EBITDA margin |
| **EBITDA multiple** | 3×–6×; add 0.5× for high NRR (>110%) and proprietary tech |
| **SDE multiple** | 2×–4× (common for owner-operated services businesses under $5M) |
| **Working capital peg** | Set at 3 months of normalized monthly expenses or median trailing 90 days—this becomes the “true-up” number at close |

### Normalization Checklist
- Owner salary above-market → add-back
- Owner car / travel / family on payroll → add-back
- One-time legal / consulting fees → add-back
- Peak-season revenue spike → normalize down
- Customer acquisition expenses historically capitalized but now not → normalize

## Deal Protections (The 5 Must-Haves)
1. **Representations & Warranties (R&W)** — specifically: revenue recognition, key customer contracts, IP ownership, no undisclosed litigation, accuracy of financials (materiality scrape = 0).
2. **Indemnification** — dollar cap at 10–15% of purchase price; basket = 0.5–1.0%; survival = 12–24 months.
3. **Working Capital True-Up** — hold back 10–15% of purchase price until 90 days post-close to true up WC peg.
4. **Earn-out protection** — if earn-out is >15% of price, cap seller’s discretion over marketing spend / pricing in earn-out period; define “gross revenue” not “net revenue.”
5. **Key Person Retention** — seller must stay 6–12 months as consultant with non-compete; if they leave early, claw back 5–10% of purchase price per month absent.

## 170-Day Integration Roadmap
| Phase | Days | Focus | Deliverable |
|-------|------|-------|-------------|
| **Pre-close** | –30 to 0 | Data room review, QoE (quality of earnings) tax drilling, customer conversations | Signed LOI, diligence report, cleanroom decision memo |
| **Stabilize** | 0–30 | Bank transitions, customer comms, payroll cut-over, emergency holdbacks | All employees on payroll; no customer churn spike; WC true-up prelim |
| **Integrate** | 30–90 | Systems merge (billing, CRM, accounting), policy alignment, roadmap decision | Single reporting stack; unified CRM; growth roadmap v1 |
| **Optimize** | 90–170 | Cross-sell if desired, cost synergies, brand decision (keep vs sunset) | Integrated P&L showing synergy capture; buyer’s credit memo |

### Integration Debt Traps to Avoid
- **Systems spaghetti** — more spent on integration than the deal was worth.
- **Cultural collision** — seller’s team quits within 90 days because buyer overrules deeply held processes.
- **Price erosion** — buyer slashes prices to “fix margins” and triggers brand-damage + churn.

## Verification: Deal Scorecard
Before committing to a small deal, every operator should score it 1–5:

| Test | Score 1 | Score 5 |
|------|---------|---------|
| Revenue growth trend | Declining >10% YoY | Consistent or improving |
| EBITDA margin | <10% | >25% |
| Customer concentration | Any customer >25% | No customer >5% |
| Recurring revenue | <50% recurring | >80% recurring |
| Founder / seller willingness to stay | Flat no / 0 months | 12+ months, non-compete |
| Integration complexity | Multiple systems, non-US ops | Single stack, domestic |
| **Total** | | **Outperform threshold: ≥24** |

If total < 24, either negotiate a 20%+ price haircut or pass. If ≥ 30, fast-track with lighter docs.

## Pitfalls
- **Seller add-backs are fiction** — always tax-return verify or back into from cash flow.
- **Losing sight of working capital peg** — failing to true-up WC can turn a “profitable deal” into a cash-out disaster.
- **Overpaying for brand** — in small deals, brand is worth 0 unless it commands pricing power you can verify in the data room.
- **Ignoring customer retention post-announcement** — announce the acquisition in a way that keeps the top 10 customers; involve the seller in those calls.
- **Legal checklist theater** — 20-point checklists create false confidence; the real test is customer interviews and cash-flow archaeology.
